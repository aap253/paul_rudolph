Discover how Paul Rudolph redefined modernism with his iconoclastic approach to architecture

Paul Rudolph (1918-97) was a visionary American architect known for his modernist design principles and innovative use of reinforced concrete. Modernist architecture chases simplicity, functionality, and rejection of ostentatious ornamentation. A subgenre of this movement is Brutalism, characterized by raw, exposed materials, bold geometric forms, and a focus on function over form. Rudolph’s commitment to concrete – a cheap, durable, and utilitarian material – cemented his role in the Brutalist movement in the mid-20th century. Even though he employed unconventional material, his work is distinguished by dramatic sculptural forms, complex spatial arrangements, and unique interpretation of light and texture.

Rudolph began his education at Auburn University and later at Harvard. He gained notoriety when he became the chair of Yale's School of Architecture (1958-65) and for designing the iconic Yale Art & Architecture Building (see in image gallery). This project particularly stands out as a Brutalist masterpiece, with its rugged surfaces and interlocking spaces. You can view some of his other notable projects in the image gallery below.

Despite controversy over his raw, expressive style and Brutalism’s decline in the late 20th century, Rudolph's impact remains undeniably groundbreaking, continuing to inspire generations of designers succeeding him.

LOMEX

In 1941, Robert Moses, the then Chairman of the Triborough Bridge and Tunnel Authority among other roles, developed the concept of the Lower Manhattan Expressway (LOMEX). He envisioned a highway cutting through Lower Manhattan that would connect Brooklyn-Queens Expressway with the West Side.

As you can imagine, locals, activists like Jane Jacobs, and community groups were deeply concerned by this project and began to vehemently oppose it by the 1960s. They feared that the expressway would displace thousands of people in SoHo and Greenwich Village, not to mention its deleterious environmental impacts.

Rudolph also opposed to project, challenging the top-down, car-centric development that disregarded human-scale, community-driven urban planning. Instead, he proposed detailed multi-level megastructures with a 10-lane elevated expressway, embedding pedestrian walkways, housing, and green spaces. His goal was to integrate residential, commercial, and transportation elements, so that urban infrastructure blended with the city rather than divide it. Rudolph’s version of LOMEX, though never built, reflected his broader architectural vision—layered, dynamic spaces that sought to humanize large-scale urban systems.

Influences, Parallels, and Legacy of Paul Rudolph

Metropolis (1927)

In Metropolis, the towering, futuristic cityscape and its bold, geometrically structured forms mirror Rudolph’s proposal of LOMEX, multi-level megastructures.

Directed by Fritz Lang

The Fountainhead (1943)

I would that Rudolph is a modern-day caricature of Howard Roark, the arrogant architect-hero in The Fountainhead. However, unlike Roark, Rudolph was more modest and had a distinctly socialist outlook.

Written by Ayn Rand

The Brutalist (2024)

Some have espoused that the protagnist of Brady Corbet's The Brutalist is an amalgamation of modernist architects like Paul Rudolph, Louis Kahn, and Marcel Breuer.
