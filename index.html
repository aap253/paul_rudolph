Ang Lee, born on October 23, 1954, in Taiwan, is a groundbreaking director. With blockbusters like Sense and Sensibility, the original Hulk, and Life of Pi, he has cemented himself as a prodigal filmmaker of modern cinema.

The movies I especially enjoy are the more "indie", "under-the-radar" ones like The Ice Storm and Brokeback Mountain that explore complex themes like identity and family dynamics in the public and private sphere. The latter film won him the Academy Award for Best Director. Even though he is an international filmmaker, he has a profound understanding of American culture, which allows him to depict American stories with honest cadency and innovative camerawork. He has an uncanny way of blending technical skills with emotional depth.

Magnum Opus?

According to me, Lee's masterpiece is Lust, Caution, an adaption of Eileen Chang's poignant espionage novel set during the Japanese occupation of China. It stars Tang Wei and Tony Leung, novice actors who quickly grew to prominence. Every time I think about this movie, the background score, Wong Chia Chi's Theme, haunts me. Attempting to pigeonhole Lee is an atrocious crime - his work spans multiude of genres and cultures. Even the renowned film critic, Roger Ebert, struggled to find the connection in Lee's filmography:

"Failing to find the connecting link between such Ang Lee films as “Sense And Sensibility,” “Brokeback Mountain” and “Hulk,” I was quickly corrected by readers who said, obviously, all his films are about people trying to realize their essential natures despite the constraints of society. Readers, you were right." – Roger Ebert
